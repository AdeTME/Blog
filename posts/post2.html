Fairytale of New York

Introduction

The way North-East out of Quebec is overseen by an odd red discolouration in the clouds. The roads are long and hypnotic, and they are lined with car dealerships and chain links of chains such as Garage, Ardene, and so on. Suburban dwellings are replicated. Parking lots are passed by the hundreds of cars on the road. The roads are plunged into complete darkness by five PM. The long nothing makes way for long stretches of woods, then factories, then more houses farther out, this time with more character and lonelier, then mills. The factories are sometimes kept within the woods. So are the houses. So are the mills. All along the highway are signs named after Saints whose names grew less seemingly canonical (Saint-Louis-du-Ha! Ha!). John Deere tractors lined the roads. Every line of green John Deere tractors was connected by Christmas lights. The lines that were not had one lone tractor that was draped by trails of bulbs, one of the sole sources of light along the road. At some point on those roads is a giant milk factory. On the roads out from Montreal and along Quebec are always large lots where gas stations reside. Every gas station is wreathed in some arrangement of the following: A&W, a Couche-Tard, St Hubert, Tim Hortons.

The latter two hours out of Quebec past Hotel Levesque at Riviere-du-Loup where everything closes at nine PM (the Maxi, however, closes at ten), it is morning and bright, exposing the previously night-obscured mountains and the Fleuve on the left of houses on hills that overlook another parking lot behind the Hotel Levesque which blares green at night across from quaint houses and sells two pieces of shrimp for ten dollars in a “resto-bar” whose tiles are bright pink and blue. The houses on the hills look down at a Tim Hortons, Super C, the Maxi that closes at ten PM, an hour later after everything else. Past the houses on the hills is Quebecois farmland, verdant, with delicate layers of snow lying atop them. Mountains rise and fall out of and in to the Earth.

Along Quebec are people who are living their lives just off of the highway. The ones whose residences are just off the highway. The ones who crawl on it to and fro work. The ones who trawl it for work. Along Quebec are highways that stretch for hours, and small locales are interspersed throughout, and they invite awe of different sentiments, but always awe nonetheless. Along Quebec is a haunting beauty, especially as the light fades and the world is only illuminated by the lights of cars and the streetlights that serve as their beacons in the wilderness of the cityscape or suburbia that is connected by the plans of people who sprawl across decades who intentionally or unintentionally atomised people out of reach.

The drive out of Quebec into Nouveau-Brunswick at eight AM is marked by driving uphill into the rising Sun. New Brunswick is announced by an hour jump forward in time and road signs that are both in English and French. A paper mill releases a valley of smoke into the sky, a polluted kingdom that floats above. The roads are sparse on either side, and whereas more than half of the way out of Quebec is lined with parking lots, most of Nouveau-Brunswick is lined with trees. Lone houses or RVs look out at the highway from within the woods. Mills are on hills. Montreal is a city that only ever implies the hilly and vertical reality of Quebec and Canada by large. The hills make themselves much more evident on the way out of Quebec, and even more so into New Brunswick.

A sign on the right reads: KNIGHT’S INN. INTERNET BY PARKING.

Short crags of rock rise up on either side of the road. In Quebec, ice snakes down them, and they are dark brown (nearly black) and white. Halfway through Nouveau-Brunswick, they’re completely dry, no ice in sight. They are sandy orange or even closer to red. They are dry brown. Whereas the greenery in Quebec had solidly moved past the Fall, on the 20th of December, New Brunswick had not yet let them go.

A sign on the right reads: MOSS HACHEY LAW. “Injured?”

Past that sign is Fredericton, where the E. John Bliss Water Treatment Plant is found on the outskirts and red, yellow, and green houses are found inside. A crosswalk is painted with the Progress Pride Flag. The buildings are relatively low to the ground and the sky is sharp blue. The most trite observation that can be made about New Brunswick: “Hi,” the gas station employees greet.

Trans Canada Highway Route Two, an hour out of Nova Scotia, two crosses: one is wreathed in pink flowers, the other in lilac. They stand tenderly aside one another.

On the way into Nova Scotia, on the left, a thin tree is cracked, keeled over and leaning on another. Just a bit further in are towering wind turbines in a large field. They are alabaster.

The Dartmouth morning is grey and Crichton Park wraps around Lake Banook. Downtown Dartmouth leads into the Woodside Area, which, like much of the city, is falling victim to gentrification. A trail cuts through the outskirts of the Woodside Area, parallel and alongside the Halifax Harbour. On the inner end of the Harbour is the Bedford Basin, where water flows in from the Sackville River, where saline water flows into the Basin, where surface water circulates outwards into the Atlantic Ocean. The Bedford Basin is where the Norwegian SS Imo departed from, where it was set to meet the SS Mont-Blanc to provide supplies to Belgium in the First World War. The SS Imo carried relief supplies, while the SS Mont-Blanc carried explosive TNT and explosive chemical compounds. The ships collided, starting a fire that culminated in an explosion that instantly killed at least one thousand six hundred people and injured thousands of others, swallowing buildings and businesses and homes. A passenger train coming in from New Brunswick was also headed for the destination of the explosion, however, Patrick Vincent Coleman who operated the railyard near the explosion sent multiple telegraphs to halt the arrival of the train. The messages Coleman sent out halted all trains headed for Halifax from completing their journeys. The railyard was vanished in the explosion, Patrick Vincent Coleman taken with it.

“Hold up the train. Ammunition ship afire in harbor making for Pier 6 and will explode. Guess this will be my last message. Good-bye boys.”

Across the water is Halifax, where the Citadel stands tall. The Citadel grounds are wide and large and grey. A door is carved within the gate to be used by the staff. The Citadel rose in 1749 when the British founded Halifax, and is demarcated by four series of forts. The fort eventually fell out of use, and in the 1940s there were lobbying efforts to demolish it, driven by downtown Halifax business interests. However, these efforts failed, and the Citadel survives now as a Parks Canada site with live historical recreations. Farther along the water is Georges Island, which historically functioned as an internment camp.

Across from the Citadel is downtown Halifax. Evident in downtown Halifax is the reality of it being a city in decay, and implicit in the attitudes of the people here and the conversations that people have is cognisance of and resignation to that fact. The decay that expresses itself seems to follow the rule of decay that many cities are expressing, which is one in which the city becomes larger and the buildings become taller and uglier, a decay defined by an industrial festering, a parasite that feeds on the character and culture of the cities that it embeds itself in. From atop the Citadel, the water is visible even as the city rises. Though the city rises, the Coastal Protection Act was passed in 2019, a culmination of efforts to preserve the coast. One of the provisions in the Act was to make it so that the buildings that rise out of downtown Halifax could not rise high enough so as to block the view of the water from the Citadel. This was not respected, and the Act was only ever passed and not proclaimed. Instead, a different version of this Act was proclaimed in 2024 by the provincial government which had no interest in such regulations. The water is visible even as the city rises, but it is relegated to gazing between tall buildings: the BMO, a CIBC, the TD. The BMO and the CIBC and the TD mostly or definitely dwarf the Halifax Town Clock erected across the Citadel to make the people of Halifax punctual. From atop the Citadel is an illuminating view of the decay. The festering rises and reaches its concrete fingers to the sky, and everyone and everything around it sinks.

The Barra MacNeils have been playing together since 1980. They tour Canada, starting in their home of Cape Breton. This year, Halifax is the 38th stop of their 25th Anniversary East Coast Christmas Tour, celebrating twenty-five years of touring Canada. Lucy MacNeil, Kyle MacNeil, Sheumas MacNeil, and Stewart MacNeil are the founding siblings, and they perform on stage with Boyd MacNeil and Ryan MacNeil, two more siblings. The concert takes place in the Dalhousie Arts Centre in a packed concert hall and starts with an acknowledgment of the turbulent nature of the year between “climate change,” “tariffs,” and “violence,” and how an event like this in the midst of that is important. The Barra MacNeils all play multiple instruments, and they fade in and out of the performance, whether by joining in on the singing or by rotating on and off the stage. The Barra MacNeils are joined by three young dancers who have also joined them from Cape Breton.

The attendants of the concert are Nova Scotians of all ages to whom this is a yearly Christmas tradition. As such, the audience is intimately familiar with the setlist, the code of conduct, and each other. Audience participation is key to the experience, and the Barra MacNeils call and rely on their participation. The concert is a range of Celtic folk music and Christmas music. In between the music, Kyle MacNeil tells stories, which sometimes bridge directly into the song that follows.

A man sits on my left at the concert. He is wearing a red nearly maroon sweater over a white shirt and tan pants. He is old with small round glasses and with deep lines in his face. He is serious and his lips are terse and thin. He is there with another woman. He is seated and he hardly moves. When he does, his lips move imperceptibly and voicelessly, and they silently sing:

“From God our Heavenly Father / A blessed Angel came,

And unto certain Shepherds / Brought tidings of the same,

How that in Bethlehem was born / The Son of God by name

O tidings of comfort and joy / Comfort and joy

O tidings of comfort and joy”

His lips also silently sing:

“Rudolph, with your nose so bright / Won’t you guide my sleigh tonight?“

His lips are moved into an affected smile at Lucy MacNeil’s rendition of Ave Maria. He sits perfectly still the entire time, save for the movements of his smile, for the quiet tapping of his feet, and for clapping at the end of songs or along to Auld Lang Syne on his feet with the entire crowd, and for the standing ovation that celebrates the Barra MacNeils.

An internal reality that has manifested within the past two years, and one that has been the fact for a very long time, is that I am being cast to the edges of myself. I enter the year with more of me than I exit it. I don’t know how much there is left. When it stops, if it stops, not much will remain.

A social reality that overcast the past two years has been one in which I have felt myself pushed farther out to the edge. From October 2023 until December 2024, that push outwards was mostly characterised by my financial reality. I righted that ship, but in the latter half of 2024, and rapidly from March in 2025 and onwards was an intense excommunication to the edges of the world I live in, in large part due to my relationship with the general public. This excommunication takes place in a city that I deeply love, that I find beautiful. Perhaps if I didn’t find it so beautiful I wouldn’t feel so dejected at its rejection.

Dartmouth and Halifax are also beautiful. They decay in a way that I find fascinating, that I feel compelled to look at, especially Halifax. That decay is cropping up in Montreal more so, though I cannot make an assessment as to how far along that decay is, or how far along it will go. It is also different to see a city before the decay and then to watch that decay happen, versus only seeing it after. (The planned demolition of the Van Horne overpass and seeing a Loto Quebec machine on the bottom floor of Centre Eaton right outside the exit from the McGill metro station are ill omens). The decay in Halifax is at a much more advanced stage.

I also find water intensely beautiful, and Halifax provides incredibly easy access to the water. The Atlantic Ocean is a twenty or thirty minute drive out from Halifax. I have not seen anything as beautiful as the Atlantic Ocean in a long time. It is a force that I felt shrive me where I stood. And out by the Atlantic Ocean, I felt for a brief moment at peace, like my self was not in discordance. I did not feel that way because whatever ails me vanished, or because my mind was clear. I was stressed as always and my mind buzzed as always. I felt at peace because I was at the edge of Canada, at the edge of the world that I know, and for once, my internal reality and my social reality were all in alignment with my geographical reality, and there is a possibility that the only place I will ever feel at peace is at the fingertips of the freezing waters of the Atlantic Ocean.

The Mic Mac Mall is instantly familiar. The Mic Mac Mall is multiple floors, it is relatively well lit courtesy of some of the ceilings being glass, it has a sunglasses store selling AI sunglasses, multiple mobile/technology stores, Winners on the bottom floor, H&M, jewelry stores, a Cinnabon on the first floor, a Taco Bell on the third, Dubai Chocolate is sold in multiple stores, the Pharmaprix is Shoppers Drug Mart, and a giant Christmas tree towers with a massive rosy round-cheeked juvenile face that peeks through the fake shrubbery and speaks to a line of children. That tree is Woody. Woody was removed from the mall in 2007, but then brought back in late 2021. Many of the women in the Mic Mac Mall wear a distinct style of make up, with bigger lips and an intense orange tint, almost England-like in its artistry. Some of the women in the Mic Mac Mall have also seemingly not escaped the tools of unskilled plastic surgeons.

At the Mic Mac Mall, there are no rules to be followed on the escalator. The right is not for standing, and the left is not for walking. It is all for standing, and there is an implied rudeness in asking someone to step aside for you. Everyone is relatively nice at the Mic Mac Mall, but everyone is relatively nice everywhere in Halifax. The Mic Mac Mall is the largest mall in Atlantic Canada, and it was named so as a mispronunciation and misspelling of the Mi’ikmaq, Native Americans who span across the Atlantic Provinces, Gaspe, and Maine. The name survived the sale of the mall and seems there to stay.

I went to the Mic Mac Mall twice. The second time I went was early in the morning, and on my way there I heard and saw crows. The second time I left, a few minutes out into the parking lot, I felt that my bag was lighter, and realised I had left my notebook on the third floor in the food court where I was taking down notes. I sped through the mall whose layout I had commit to memory by that point. By the time I had gotten to the food court again I had resigned myself to the fact that the only proof that I have existed for the past few months was now in the garbage shared by the Subway and Thai Express and A&W and Jugo Juice and the New York Fries and the people who were at the Mic Mac Mall from 9:30AM when it opened until 10:45 AM when I had left. Luckily, my notebook was there undisturbed. I left again, and I heard and saw more crows.

I have felt something akin to a constant headache for the past two years. The only analgesic that has somewhat helped has been looking away, looking down, narrowing my vision, a darker environment in my apartment. I have felt something akin to that headache outside in the past two years. It was one I felt because of my increasing inability to deal with being perceived and the implications of perception, because of a neurosis that has been slipping the tight reins which I keep it on. On the 28th of November I found out I have to leave Montreal and so something akin to a constant headache changed nature and simultaneously completely spilled over into my apartment. I can’t look at the world or at my walls or the tree outside the window. I can’t be in my apartment. The headache now pulses with this reminder: “It is soon to be lost.”

Upon arriving in Nova Scotia, I experienced a hint of that same idyllic idealism I had when I first arrived in Montreal in 2019: the notion that I could leave my core problems behind. I had not packed them in my bag, and since I did not, they were left in my apartment. In 2019, I did not pack them in my bag and left them in the house I grew up in, continents away. Even though I knew my visit was barely a week, that possibility lit up in my mind for a moment. For the first forty-eight hours or so that I was in Dartmouth, I entertained the notion that leaving Montreal would not be as cataclysmic as it will be. I could leave and my head would be fine again, that maybe it would just fall back into the same old pains of loops or the paralysis of perception, familiar headaches which I could deal with, if only for the fact that they had become a part of day-to-day life. For those few days, something akin to a headache had reversed to its previous more familiar nature.

I left on the twentieth and arrived on the twenty-first. On the twenty-fourth, its nature reversed again around 11AM. It was a discompassionate reminder that the discordance cannot be simply left behind. Much of it is caused from the outside world, and just as much is caused from within. There is no need to pack it. I first learned this lesson when I was seventeen or eighteen, walking near the houses a few blocks over near Hingston Hall A on Concordia’s Loyola Campus. At twenty-five, after seventy-two or so hours of forgetting that lesson, I received it again on a walk back up Crichton Park away from the Mic Mac Mall.

The way West out of Nova Scotia is abandoned. The Sun is swallowed by grey blue. The cars are driverless. The letters on the signs are incomprehensible. Winter has seized the roads. The trees are in death. Long tarps of snow blanket the sides of the road.

A train crosses the highway in Nouveau-Brunswick. A cross and a wreath are on the side of the road, on the right. A sign on the right, like many on the left, across provinces, reads: IRVING. CLEAN WASHROOMS AHEAD.

A sign on the right with an ice cream cone topped with a vanilla scoop and pink frosting reads: My Own Famous Homemade Recipe. Three songs later, in the chorus of So Long, Marianne (“Now so long, Marianne, it’s time that we began / To laugh and cry and cry and laugh about it all again”) an elevated sign of the same ice cream cone points into the woods on the left.

A sign on the right reads: Welcome to New Maryland. New Brunswick’s Last Fatal Duel….. and Resting Place. Underneath that, EXIT 285 A. Two pistols are crossed over each other on the sign.

A gas station sign reads: DENNY’S 25 PERCENT OFF FOR FIRST RESPONDERS. In the Fen’s Market adjacent to the gas station, the cashier is a young man, and he likes POiSON GiRLFRiEND, specifically MELTING MOMENT. The crags on either side now have veins of ice coursing down them. Further along the Nouveau-Brunswick roads remains the factory which still builds that valley of smoke above the town in which it resides. A wispy floating kingdom yet to collapse.

Grand Falls is a quaint town on the edge of New Brunswick, and it has the following procession of parallel streets: Rue Chapel Street, Rue Sheriff Street, and Broadway Boulevard. A massive graveyard is on the left, headstones and crosses rising up on the side of a hill. Outside one of the coloured homes is a green inflatable T-Rex with a red giftbox in its mouth, and up the icy roads of Grand Falls is a waterfall, frozen over, though some streams have avoided that fate and they flow somewhat rapidly. They flow in the direction of a gorge, a scar left from an ancient stake that was driven into the earth and split rocky snow-covered iced-over formations of rock on either side, and that gorge is overlooked by the Grand Falls Generating Station, a hydro electric dam that overlooks the drop into the ledges of rock and down into the frozen water. The McDonald’s in Grand Falls promises the following: “We’re hiring you for you.” The McDonald’s in Grand Falls is mainly manned by a prepubescent cherubic young boy.

The drive out of Quebec was into the rising Sun, and the drive back in is into the setting Sun. On the right, the Fleuve peeks through the trees that line the hillside, and eventually it stretches azure and ivory, cleaving Quebec all throughout, and the rocks leading up to the marsh leading up to the Fleuve are topped white with snow and glistening with ice, and the setting Sun shines off of the snow by the Fleuve, and the Fleuve glints blue and the Sky is amber. Mountains line the horizon. Near the Fleuve in Kamouraska and on the east of the Appalachians is La Pocatiere, a small Quebecois town with soft-coloured houses and home to the formerly-named Musée François-Pilote, now the Musée québécois de l’agriculture et de l’alimentation, a museum of Quebec ethnology. The night grows deeper farther into Quebec, into Riviere-du-Loup, into Trois-Rivieres, but it gets brighter with light pollution, the lights of cars and the massive gas station lots. The paper factory still covers the sky in smoke. A truck is dipped into a cavity on the left side of the road, skidmarks left in its wake.

A sign on the right reads: Mort de fatigue. Arrêtez-vous avant!

The reentry into Montreal is hollow and still. The solitary houses and the factories and the mills and chains of chains and car dealerships and the cars and the signs and the gas stations are gone. The streets are gone and the easily-missable turns are gone. Pont Jacques-Cartier is gone. Lafontaine Park is gone. The apartment on 3945 Saint Andre is gone, too.

The only way for me to deal with the fact that this is my last Christmas living in Montreal was by spending it in Dartmouth. The last Christmas that I spend in Montreal is in 2024. In that week I am mostly on Avenue Lauriel. I nap face-to-face with my friend’s cat. She is white and orange and affectionate and communicative and lays her small paw on my cheek and has no reservations as to how close she puts her face to anyone’s. Those blissful blue evenings are in the rearview. That memory is safe harbour. It is a momentary spot of refuge. It is safe harbour from the year prior to it, and in the visage of that Christmas week shines a promise at the shore, not safe harbour but a sanctuary by the water. It is a sanctuary that never was. It is a promise no one ever kept. It is a promise no one ever made. Dartmouth was too safe harbour from the failure and grief that is here and immediate.

Now, the year ends. There were never sanctuaries behind. There are no sanctuaries ahead.
