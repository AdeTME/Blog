Luigi Mangione: On Martyrdom, or Living On a Corpse

Introduction

2024 was not a year that was short of headlines and stories that captured the cultural zeitgeist. Some prominent ones that I can think of are Israel’s detonation of the Hezbollah pagers, the attempted assassination of Donald Trump, and Kamala Harris announcing that she was running for President. To lesser extents, the murder of Nasrallah or the deposition of Bashar Al-Assad by Syrian rebels were also grabbed the attention of many. Bashar being ousted is probably the most surprising event of the year, in my opinion, but it definitely wasn’t the one to garner the most attention. The common thread between these events/stories is that they were all stories that remained relatively relevant in an era of extremely rapid turnover in terms of the news events that people see and then hold in their mind. There is, however, one story that I think captured the attention of many and remained (though it now wanes) in the eyes and minds of many in ways that very few, if any other stories in 2024, did at all. That is, of course, Luigi Mangione’s assassination of the CEO of UnitedHealthcare, Brian Thompson.

“The Facts”

These are the facts and timeline that I am working with:

On December 4th, in New York, from roughly 5:40AM-6:30AM, Luigi Mangione (obviously then an unidentified shooter) roamed near the Hilton hotel where he would shoot Brian Thompson. At approximately 6:45AM, Mangione shoots Thompson multiple times. His gun would jam, he would fix it, then he would shoot an already injured Thompson again. Luigi flees the scene and gets on a bike and rides away, while Thompson would be found by the police at 6:48AM. A ditched backpack is then found by the NYPD who believed that it was Mangione’s. Mangione, on the other hand, was gone.

Then, on December 9th, Mangione would be recognised by an employee at a McDonald’s in Pennsylvannia (a picture of him with the mask down and had been released to the public) and reported to the police. He would be subsequently detained, found with a gun, a fake ID that matched the one that he had used to check into a hostel in Manhattan, and his handwritten manifesto, which is then circulated to Ken Klippenstein, who leaks it on December 10th. On December 17th, Mangione would receive additional charges, and on December 20th, the infamous perp walk alongside a dozen or so cops and New York Mayor, Eric Adams.

He is now being held in Brooklyn’s Metropolitan Detention Center, where he awaits trial.

PART ONE

On Martyrdom

Activity/Passivity

Now, given the title of this article, it is easy to draw the conclusion that I think that Luigi Mangione is a martyr. That is correct. I think he performed an act of martyrdom. I also do not say this to say whether or not he is a hero. I use the term of martyr relatively neutrally here. I think the main reason that Luigi enraptured the hearts of many is because we do live in an era of few significant martyrs, despite us placing a very deep importance on martyrdom as a concept. Martyrs are very integral to religion, for example. Jesus’ martyrdom on the cross is arguably the defining moment for Christianity and Christian ethics. Islam also places a very heavy emphasis on martyrdom. For one, martyrdom is practically a sure-way ticket to heaven in Islamic theology. Our cultural outputs, our stories, and our values all tend to venerate martyrdom. Luigi is a modern martyr that captured people’s hearts and minds in a way that I don’t think anyone has in a long time.

One thing that I am aware of is that there is a heavy intersection between the victims of conflict and the notions of martyrdom, and that my claim that there are not many modern martyrs could be seen as dismissive of the plights of many. For example, many would call all Palestinians martyrs because of their persecution at the hands of Israel. That could be extended to Ukrainians at the hands of Russia, or Sudanese people at the hands of their government. However, I would argue that most of these people are not martyrs (at least in what I think is my broad understanding of the word) because I think do not think that their condition is necessarily reflected in the different aspects of martyrdom (in my conception), chief among them being activity versus passivity.

Zeroing in on Palestinians for a moment, the reasons I would say that many of them are not martyrs is because of the passivity of their situations. A massive distinction between a martyr and a victim, in my opinion, is that a martyr often volunteers their persecution, whereas a victim is not someone who does so, but rather is made to endure it passively. When I say passively, I do not for a second mean to imply that the average Palestinian is not doing anything about their situations. The Palestinian condition is defined by constant instability and turbulence, especially in the past year. Constantly having to move out of zones which are being bombed, then moving back in, then moving back out. The notion of a “home” erodes and is undermined when, any day, you can receive flyers or warnings that that home might become rubble soon, if that warning ever comes.

Clearly, the average Palestinian civilians’ condition then is not one which is passive at all: it is mentally active (the stress of truly having no guarantee whether you, your family, or friends are going to be alive tomorrow), and physically active (constantly moving from place to place, especially in times of warfare). But this then becomes passive, and is ultimately why I view it as more victimhood than martyrdom, because that is just the way of life that they are subjected to. This is their day-to-day. It is no way to live, and they have no other choice. It’s basically the same as you commuting to work, as you making dinner, as you going out for a drink every Saturday.

This of course does not for a second mean that Palestinians are short of martyrs. There absolutely are martyrs, such as doctors or journalists - practically anyone who could feasibly leave, but chooses not to. Some of these doctors or journalists are celebrated/mourned when martyred — Shireen Abu Akleh comes to mind here — but I would say that most really aren’t. In Palestinian society itself, martyrdom is a very prevalent concept, and many do conceive every Palestinian death as a martyr for the cause of Palestinian resistance. But not every Palestinian, in my opinion, is living for the cause of Palestinian resistance. Many of them are just trying to survive. And maybe that in and of itself is a form of resistance. I don’t want to delve into this too much, but in the framework I have now, I view Palestinian civilians as victims of conflicts more than they are martyrs because I view designating victims of conflict as martyrs to possibly undermine the grave nature of what they undergo.

When thinking of martyrdom in this way, one thing that I have to try and reason then is whether any person who signs up for any army would be considered a martyr. I think it’s complicated and hard to say. Martyrdom includes choice, specifically a choice to give up either all of your quality of life or your life itself, because of something you believe in. If you truly believed that Saddam had weapons of mass destruction, and you signed up to go fight in Iraq and prevent what you view to be a cataclysmic threat to your way of life (or your very life itself), are you not a martyr? I think then we start to zero in a bit on another aspect of martyrdom: the fact that it usually is defined by one single moment, often a moment that the martyr knows is very likely to spell a condemnation or renouncement of one’s life. Jesus on the cross, and Thích Quảng Đức or Mohammad Bouazizi’s self-immolations. This could apply to soldiers as the moment they deploy, but I would argue to some extent that they almost enter into a passive state where their way of life changes. Obviously it is still in pursuit of a greater goal while giving up a lot. Soldiers are practically signing their lives away, and if they survive, they often sacrifice a normalcy that does seem to more often than not never return. I think this passivity, and also uncertainty with regards to their fate, does differentiate the soldier from the self-immolating monk. The soldier doesn’t know what will come next — the monk knows he is to die.

This does obviously raise the question of whether Luigi Mangione “knew” he would get caught. I don’t want to say that he knew he would get caught, but I think he was pretty aware that he would. For as much as everyone speculated that he was a “professional” and that “he would never be found,” I think doing something that he did in the era that we live in — one of advanced levels of surveillance — he was probably aware that he was much more likely to be caught than not. It’s also important to note that this is only an element to martyrdom, and it can be less than or greater than the other aspects of martyrdom. I think I’ve said all there is to say about activity and passivity here, and differentiated some of the parameters of martyrdom, both in my opinion but also from what I observe in the ways that people consciously (and subconsciously) conceptualise, depict, and talk about martyrdom.

Definitional/Resonant

A point that also needs to be addressed is how martyrdom and violence intersect. Luigi obviously used violence. So does martyrdom necessitate a lack of violence? Well, clearly a self-immolation is an act of violence, though self-inflicted. Jesus’ martyrdom was an act of violence, though external, not one he committed on someone else. So what happens when Luigi kills a man? Well, it’s not his act of violence itself that defines the martyrdom, but the consequence. He did initially run away, and as I just mentioned, it’s not as if he knew for certain he would get caught. But he did also basically give up everything he had, and condemned himself to a life on the run had he not been caught.

I think one thing to consider is that maybe there exists two planes to martyrdom, then. The planes are objective martyrdom — that I will call definitional martyrdom — and then a subjective martyrdom, or what I will call a resonant martyrdom. Definitional martyrdom to me is the martyrdom that I would apply to, say, an ISIS suicide bomber. I can recognise that definitionally a suicide bomber is probably (and is, as uncomfortable as it is to say) a martyr, only insofar as you can strip the normative loading of the term martyrdom and just look into how they fit in the (or at least my) conception of martyrdom. At the very least, I can recognise why he views himself as a martyr, even if I don’t agree with the cause or the methods. I also feel like martyrdom is a little bit amorphous and vague, and I do feel like it really does kind of depend on the fusion and interaction of different elements such as the elements of activity and passivity I mentioned, or the elements of reception and weight that will follow this section. However, an ISIS suicide bomber’s martyrdom does not resonate with me on any level. But Jesus’ martyrdom, and especially Bouazizi’s martyrdom, resonate deeply with me. This leads to me then ultimately not calling someone who commits an act of martyrdom because of a cause I don’t believe in (for example, establishing a global caliphate) or using methods I don’t agree with (harming others, only to an extent) a martyr, but I can comfortably call Luigi’s act of violence an act of martyrdom because, as it has with many others, resonated deeply. Those acts are acts of resonant martyrdom.

Reception/Weight

I think one thing that became very clear to me from the Mangione case is that martyrdom is not equal, especially in the way that it is received by others and the perceived weight that it carries. You’ll notice that earlier I mentioned two very prominent self-immolations, but I left out the most recent one that got somewhat significant coverage: that being Aaron Bushnell’s self-immolation. This is because one of the most crucial aspects to martyrdom is reception. Martyrdom is very dependent on its reception by people. Thích Quảng Đức’s self-immolation was one of the most significant moments in the collapse of the Diệm regime and was instantly canonised in our collective cultural mind’s eye, with the picture of the immolation being one of the most important pictures of the 20th century. Bouazizi’s immolation sparked the Arab Spring, a series of anti-government demonstrations that erupted across the Middle East and North Africa in a way that had never been seen before. Despite largely being a failure (in my opinion), the Arab Spring very much was a moment that implied much about the repressed desire for freedom that is very deeply buried within the MENA psyche. There were of course other reasons that Diệm’s regime collapsed or that the Arab Spring happened, but many look at these two self-immolations as turning points for their respective associated historical events.

But, sadly — and harshly — Bushnell was nothing but a blip on the radar. Bushnell’s self-immolation, in my opinion, cannot even be compared to Bouazizi’s self-immolation because it really ultimately did nothing. Most people I know could not even tell you Bushnell’s name. They probably could not tell you Bouazizi’s name, of course, but they might know of him, or if they don’t, you can on a relatively objective level point to Bouazizi’s self-immolation and then connect it to an outcome (that being the Arab Spring). However, what outcome could you connect Bushnell to? Nothing of significance. There are many reasons for this. The first is probably that there is very little that could probably be done for the United States to divest from Israel. The most that they could do is exert pressure to try and keep Israel on a leash. This was practised by the Biden administration, such as when he threatened to withold weapons from Israel if they invaded Rafah. But Israel is too important to the US as its main ally in the Middle East.

The second is reception, which is how you respond to a potential act of martyrdom. Reception meaning how you engage with said act, how it makes you feel, if it sticks with you long term or not. Reception is very similar to resonance to the point of almost sounding like it’s the same thing. The big difference to me is that I think reception doesn’t happen before resonance is resolved. Something can be received without resonating, but I don’t think that it can be received before you have resolved whether or not it resonates, because it helps guide whether you will even receive it and how you will receive it. Resonance is also a bit more broad, whereas reception is more specific. Resonance would be thinking Luigi committed an act of martyrdom that personally connected with you, and reception could be writing an article about it. People received Thích Quảng Đức’s self-immolation and John F. Kennedy would say that “no news picture in history has generated so much emotion around the world as that one,” which obviously implies a resonance too. Bouazizi’s self-immolation was received by Arabs who saw that as the last spark to go out and do something about their conditions.

Bushnell’s self-immolation definitely struck some people. It struck me. But not for long, really. There’s a reason this article isn’t about Bushnell. Bushnell’s martyrdom might have resonated somewhat for me, but I do not think I necessarily received it (so engaged with it or felt a really impassioned way about it) in the way that someone like Bouazizi did, especially because I knew that it would be incredibly meaningless because of the deadlock that is Israel-Palestine. And I don’t think most people in the US were necessarily too affected by it because Israel-Palestine isn’t exactly what most Americans are thinking about day-in day-out, if ever.

The people who I think engaged with Bushnell’s martyrdom, or were at all affected by it, are probably people who already care about Palestine, or are leaning in that direction. Arabs were obviously stirred emotionally by Bouazizi because he was not only one of them (in as much as a broader Pan-Arab identity is held by people), but also because they could sympathise with him because they lived under the same types oppressive regimes. But can someone who lives in America really, in their heart of hearts, sympathise to the same extent with Palestinians? To some extent, but ultimately no, not really. You can only put yourself in someone’s shoes so much until you are faced with the gap of lived experience. It’s a conflict that’s happening so far away, to people you have little in common with. Bushnell obviously did have an ocean of sympathy, but he was extraordinary. The average person definitionally isn’t.

And in an era where images are so ethereal and the mind is constantly blasted by data and a cacophony of images, can an image really take hold in the way Thích Quảng Đức’s picture did in the 1960’s? It has to be a truly exceptional image, or a truly exceptional narrative a la Mangione. One that I can think of is the picture of the Syrian child — Aylan Kurdi — who washed up on a beach in Turkey. But that was also in 2016, and I would argue that the impermanence of images has only increased since. Ultimately, Bushnell’s self-immolation was largely — and tragically — for nothing.

This doesn’t mean that Bushnell’s self-immolation was not a significant act in and of itself, and that there isn’t a level of heroism and true human compassion there. There is — way more than the average person possesses — and definitely way more than I possess. To see what the Palestinian people are going through, and see all the pictures and the videos… It makes sense to self-immolate after that. Not that I think it’s necessarily productive, or “smart,” but I think responding in this way when you exist in a world that allows such injustice makes sense. But when something is called significant, it tends to be with relation to something. So can you call something significant in and of itself when a dependent factor (some form of outcome) is missing? And I do not think that martyrdom is necessarily done for an outcome, to be clear. It can, most simply, an outcome of one’s expression of one’s grievances at a perceived injustice, one that often involves paying a grave price for one’s beliefs. But sadly, there is a level in which I have to look at it in that “outcome” lens. It’s hard to see a man set himself on fire, and then watch as practically nothing comes from it.

Something that ties into reception very concretely is the idea of the “weight” of something. What is a person giving up when they become a martyr? Giving up a life seems to be the greatest thing you could give up, but there is this calculation being made as to what kind of life is being given up? After Bushnell’s death, his Reddit posts were found, under the username acebush1. Many of the posts that that account made align very closely to Bushnell’s life, and that the Twitch User ID number that was linked to Bushnell’s Twitch account — LillyAnarKitty, where he livestreamed his self-immolation — was formerly also named acebush1. On his Reddit account, he would post laments of his financial troubles, and regrets with regards to joining the military. Contrast this with Luigi’s personal life, his looks, his coming from wealth, the fact that he was an engineer who graduated from an Ivy League school, and you begin to see why it can seem like Luigi gave up more, even though he remains alive, while Bushnell died a painful death.

This is not to say that Bushnell’s life “means less,” and that even if it did, that would make what he did mean less… But it almost does, at least in the way that his martyrdom was received. And to make an uncomfortable confession, when I found out more about Bushnell, one of my first thoughts was: “Yeah, makes sense.” And I think that that is definitely part of why he was received in an ultimately milquetoast way. This finally then leads to tackling the question:

Why Luigi?

To answer this question, I think something that might be very helpful is to look at the narratives that surrounded Luigi before his capture. The first was surrounding his identity. Many people online theorised that Mangione was a professional hitman because of how “composed” he was during the assassination. Others, and what was the more prevailing theory, theorised that he was someone who was aggrieved by American healthcare because he was personally screwed over by it whether directly or indirectly — as in he was denied healthcare, or if it was someone he knew (father, mother, girlfriend) being denied healthcare. Of these two phantom narratives, I would say that the latter was the prevailing one. And it’s not an odd one to manifest. I think the first one was a bit more juvenile, insofar as it seems guided by movies and TV shows, but the second one really makes sense because the instant conclusion that people would draw is that this person must have been personally wronged by the system, and so enacted violence upon it. This inaccurate narrative, in my opinion, is very key to why Luigi not only captured the attention of many, but garnered the level of sympathy that he did.

The first part of it is, without a doubt, not what I just mentioned, but it is that he is attractive. It feels silly to write, but it is (somewhat sadly), the truth. The Internet’s reaction to Luigi when that first picture of him was released was to rave over his looks. I think part of it is because people don’t really expect shooters to be attractive. It was not what people imagined him to look like, and the fact that he looked much better led to his looks very quickly becoming a very focal point to the conversation, for better or for worse. In my opinion, it is a little bit of both. On the one hand, I think that him being attractive definitely helps him, if for only the fact that it drew more attention. It not only boosted his popularity, but it also undermined some of the angles that some of the media tried to spin about him — that he had committed an act of incel rage, since even though he admitted he had issues being intimate because of his back problems, no one took that seriously, especially given how much people on the internet fawned over him. On the other hand, it redirects the conversation to a very unserious discussion and engagement with him. Far be it from me to claim that I haven’t fawned over his looks, of course, or made jokes about it, but I think the fact that that very quickly became the focal centre is incredibly unfortunate.

The second part, which I alluded to earlier, is that Luigi was someone who had a lot going for him, but made a decision to functionally give it all up. An attractive young man, who everyone spoke of highly, who came from a rich family, an Ivy League engineering graduate, decided to throw all of that away. Obviously, as I mentioned already, I cannot say that he knew for certain he would get caught. The next best thing was that he was going to spend the rest of his life on the run. Such a high profile assassination of a rich man was absolutely going to garner a lot of attention, and subsequently a lot of resources to find him. This, I think, ties into either the conscious and unconscious calculation that I believe people make when they evaluate a martyr. By the metrics of what we place importance on, Luigi had it all. And to watch him throw it away, I believe, sparked something in people.

The third part is how selfless of an act it seems Luigi had committed. It extends even beyond selfless: it is almost entirely patriotic. When you look at the prevailing phantom narrative I mentioned earlier, it is an inherently selfish one. It assumed that he was personally denied healthcare, or that someone he personally knew was. This narrative did make an assumption about his health and his psyche, which we actually do have some information about.

From the information we do have on hand, Luigi’s health profile, especially leading up to the assassination, was actually improving. While he would mention having brain fog, and that he had just undergone lower back surgery because of chronic pain (things which he had posted about on his Reddit and X accounts), he would report that he was doing much better and was feeling no pain. Eventually, the posts would stop, and he would go missing for months leading up to the assassination, and people close to him even reached out to him on his social media. Anyway, there is nothing right now that points towards the healthcare system specifically, directly, harming Luigi. While healthcare in America can still screw over rich people, there is no indication that Mangione was personally affected in terms of being denied care, or in terms of the financial issues that can arise when seeking care, and nor was he even insured by UnitedHealthcare. More than that, he doesn’t even mention his own health issues at all in his manifesto, nor allude to being personally affected — outside of the broader context of all Americans being affected — by healthcare.

I’ve mentioned the manifesto a few times now. His manifesto was leaked by Ken Klippenstein, seen below:

“To the Feds, I'll keep this short, because I do respect what you do for our country. To save you a lengthy investigation, I state plainly that I wasn't working with anyone. This was fairly trivial: some elementary social engineering, basic CAD, a lot of patience. The spiral notebook, if present, has some straggling notes and To Do lists that illuminate the gist of it. My tech is pretty locked down because I work in engineering so probably not much info there. I do apologize for any strife of traumas but it had to be done. Frankly, these parasites simply had it coming. A reminder: the US has the #1 most expensive healthcare system in the world, yet we rank roughly #42 in life expectancy. United is the [indecipherable] largest company in the US by market cap, behind only Apple, Google, Walmart. It has grown and grown, but as our life expectancy? No the reality is, these [indecipherable] have simply gotten too powerful, and they continue to abuse our country for immense profit because the American public has allwed them to get away with it. Obviously the problem is more complex, but I do not have space, and frankly I do not pretend to be the most qualified person to lay out the full argument. But many have illuminated the corruption and greed (e.g.: Rosenthal, Moore), decades ago and the problems simply remain. It is not an issue of awareness at this point, but clearly power games at play. Evidently I am the first to face it with such brutal honesty.”

The manifesto is a bit over 260 words, give or take, since some of it is not legible. It’s very straightforward, and it really does illustrate why Luigi resonated so well. While it is obviously political, it appeals to patriotic/populist notions, rather than being placeable on a left/right spectrum. This gets to the heart of something about Luigi that is absolutely missing for Bushnell. Despite killing a man, he has largely not been received as an alienating figure. When news of the shooting first broke, many on the left celebrated him as a leftist hero, being a man who just killed a CEO of an insurance company. Praxis in practise, at last. But when you read the first line of his manifesto, you can instantly tell that is not the case. Very few leftists, if any, would start their manifesto by mentioning their respect for the Feds and what they do for their country. There is a palpable patriotism and pro-Americanism that permeates throughout his manifesto, something which people on the right definitely resonated with.

Moreover, after Mangione’s identity was revealed, people combed through his Reddit and his X posts, as well as his Good Reads. One thing that instantly became clear was that there was no unifying theme for his politics. He was not a leftist hero, and he was not some right wing hero either. He does absolutely express a level of anti-capitalism, seeing as he advised people on Reddit to lie and claim that an injury would prevent them from working to get the healthcare they need, writing: “We live in a capitalist society, I’ve found that the medical industry responds to these key words far more urgently than you describing unbearable pain and how it’s impacting your quality of life.” Clearly, he expresses a distaste for capitalism here. However, anti-capitalism is not necessarily anti-populist, and can in fact be quite compatible. It also makes it harder to pin him as a Democrat or Republican, as those parties are generally both pro-capitalist parties.

When Bushnell died, on the other hand, and his Reddit account was parsed through, it was revealed he was an anarchist/far-leftist. These are incredibly unpopular opinions with the average American. He was also incredibly anti-American, but not in the way that I would argue a lot of Republicans are nowadays: Bushnell being an anarchist who wanted the collapse of America, while Republicans have a very un-American conception of America, but a conception of America nonetheless.

And while there is a very strong Pro-Palestinian movement amongst young people, especially on college campuses, this is not reflective at all of the average American. When you look at exit polling for the 2024 US election, 31% of Americans felt that support for Israel was too strong, while 31% thought it was not strong enough, and 30% thought it was just right. When you look at most Americans, I think the most “progressive” sentiment that is present is an increasing demand for a ceasefire (at the time of the linked poll a ceasefire had not been reached yet), or at least sympathy for the victims of Israeli aggression. But most American do not blame their government in the way that Bushnell does. At most, Americans are mixed on the issue, and they clearly do not feel as strongly about it nor as condemnatory about it as Bushnell does. Hence, a lack of resonance.

Back to the manifesto, it becomes clear that Mangione did what he did because he was impassioned to act for his fellow Americans. His manifesto is even apologetic. He keeps it short to not waste the time of the Feds, he apologises for “any strife of traumas.” He then makes it clear that he views people like Thompson as people who are very directly benefiting off of the suffering of Americans, when he dispassionately says: “Frankly, these parasites simply had it coming.” He laments how despite how expensive healthcare is, Americans are not reaping benefits which reflect that price. An incredibly interesting part of the manifesto, as well, is that he believes that while yes, these people are parasites exploiting America, that the American public “has allwed them to get away with it.” He assigns a level of responsibility to the inaction of people. He follows this by admitting that the problem is complex, but that ultimately he needed to do something about it, because he believes that he can no longer allow Americans to be gouged in the way that they are by healthcare. Mangione, despite walking up to a man and shooting him, garnered a lot more sympathy than a man who self-immolated for people thousands of kilometres away. And it’s because, ultimately, he did it for them. Hence, a profound resonance.

Luigi Mangione spurred into action, turning his passive exploitation by the healthcare system (as an American) into an act of martyrdom that resonated with many, and clearly held a lot of weight. But what about the reception? And possibly just as importantly, what about the outcome?

PART TWO

Living On a Corpse

Three Miscalculations

Reception and outcome are often inextricably tied. Bouazizi, as I mentioned earlier, was received by Arabs with a great sympathy that inflamed them to — along other factors — carry out a series of uprisings. The Arab Spring itself, just the fact that it happened, was an outcome. And then that outcome led to another outcome, which was the failure of any manifestation of a proper democracy in the Middle East and North Africa. The closest was Tunisia, which inevitably backslid. And while I look back on the Arab Spring as having, on a very real level, failed, I do also look at it as a moment that implied the Arab psyche had a yearning for freedom, buried very deep inside by decades of authoritarian regimes who very intentionally have done their best to put out that flame.

When we look at how Mangione was received, it is important to look at not only how people received him, but how the system itself did. Now, how did “the system” receive Mangione? By “the system,” I mean the news media, the police, politicians, the ruling capitalist class, etc. I think there was absolutely a level of shock that was delivered to it. A lone gunman walks up to a CEO, and shoots him dead. It was as quick and as simple as that. This required a strategic response, and I think we saw two main directions.

Prior to knowing anything about Mangione, there was a near instant deification or mythologisation. There was almost no way for Mangione to meet the narratives that I think people had set out. But he completely surpassed those narratives, in my opinion. This was not something that I think the system, specifically the media, was expectin. And and so came the first main direction: undermining Mangione. There was an incredibly concerted effort by many news organisations to try and destroy his character. They initially refused to release anything about the manifesto and just alluding to him being a deranged, lone gunman, and when the manifesto was made public, cherry-picking parts of the manifesto and alluding/outright calling him insane. However, none of this worked. Mangione’s manifesto leaked, only garnering more sympathy, and people’s opinions of him hardly changed after the smear campaigns. All across the mainstream media, there was just very little effort in trying to understand why Luigi did what he did and where he was coming from. As he said, “It’s completely out of touch, and an insult to the intelligence of the American people, and their lived experience!”

The second main strategy against Mangione is to make an example of him. I think the first part — a miscalculation — was releasing so many images of Mangione. After his arrest, we were bombarded with pictures of Mangione: in his cell and in his handcuffs. Pictures that were provided by/released by police. This, of course, was an incredibly gross miscalculation, as with each picture, people would only fawn over Mangione more. It got so bad that the New York Times actually changed course, instead (according to Ken Klippenstein) ordering a moratorium on releasing so many pictures of Luigi, as they realised it only garnered further support. This same logic (along with not wanting to inspire more shootings of this kind) is almost definitely the reason why his manifesto was not released.

Then, there was the infamous perp walk. Mangione’s perp walk was host to roughly a dozen cops, some with rifles, and the Mayor of New York, Eric Adams, who is being charged for federal corruption charges (bribery and illegally taking money from foreign nationals), and was seemingly very inflamed at Mangione. Adams said it himself, anyway: “I wanted to send a strong message with the police commissioner that we’re leading from the front. I’m not going to just allow him to come into our city. I wanted to look him in the eye to say that, ‘You carried out this terrorist act in my city, the city that the people of New York love,’ and I wanted to be there to show the symbolism of that.” A generous reading of that would be that that message is a condemnation of political violence, which I think is fair to feel about Mangione. But I do find it hard to believe that Eric Adams’ reason for wanting to send a strong message is specifically for that. This is, after all, a man who allowed a skyscraper to be built in the city he claims to so deeply love without a fire inspection because it suited the interests of his benefactors. So, I do think it is entirely more likely that he is not fond of that kind of violence against the ruling class, and this unnecessary show of force was meant to strike fear.

This, again, was obviously a gross miscalculation, as people would only further glamourise and mythologise Mangione. He was being treated extraordinarily, and in doing so, it only sought to make him seem more extraordinary. I suspect that the way to make an example of him that will actually have its intended effect will probably be his sentencing. Mangione was met with charges of first degree murder, murder as an act of terrorism, stalking, and more, all of which could ultimately culminate in a death penalty — all charges which he plead “not guilty” to . I have no legal background or experience to be able to comment on why he plead not guilty. I could imagine them maybe arguing a case for self-defense, seeing as Brian Thompson, in Luigi’s view, is aggressing on him and the American people. Regardless, I would imagine that that case will resolve with Luigi being put away for a long time.

It seems, then, that Luigi is somewhat victorious. He was undermined, but it didn’t work. They are making an example out of him, and he will almost definitely be locked away in jail for a very long time. But even then, he remains a hero in the eyes of many. All in all, this should be a relatively positive outcome. But it isn’t. Because we live in a system that breeds and inspires meaninglessness.

A Nihilistic System

I argue that outcome is an incredibly important part of martyrdom. So what was the outcome of Mangione’s martyrdom? Bringing back the Arab Spring parallel, while I do not think that Mangione will spark anything close to the revolutions that erupted then (revolutions that obviously happened for a multitude of reasons), he did absolutely start a conversation and shed a light on what people are feeling. The fact that people across the political aisle expressed either glee or complete apathy at the cold-blooded killing of a man in the street is incredibly telling as to how people feel towards the ruling class, and people like Thompson who make their wealth and cement their positions in said class off of the backs of Americans. But many on the right who sympathise with Mangione simultaneously voted in a man who is currently establishing a billionaire run tech oligarchy. While I mentioned that Mangione resonated with Americans across the political spectrum because he acted on something that many Americans sympathise with, it is also clear that a lot of people suffer from a high level of cognitive dissonance.

Moreover, Thompson was just one man. He will simply get replaced by one of many people whose psychologies have been so deeply warped by capitalism that they are willing to exploit other human beings and deny them healthcare. UnitedHealthcare will not change its policies. The American healthcare system will not change. And I don’t think Luigi expected to topple the system by killing Thompson, nor lead a revolution. I think in admitting that the problem is a lot more complex than he lays out, he recognises that the solution is a lot more complex than facing the situation as “brutally honestly” as he did. He could have just been expressing a grievance, as I mentioned earlier that that can be something martyrdom is used for.

But… Now what? I will preface this by conceding one thing entirely. The question “Now what?” is one that might misunderstand Mangione, or maybe even martyrdom as a whole. It’s one that betrays a utilitarian, outcome-based thinking. There are shades of this all through this article. It greatly informs how I feel and talk Bushnell. And again, it’s not that Bushnell’s actions weren’t significant, but they were ultimately made meaningless. That does feel like where Mangione is headed to, if he is not already there. And I can’t help but think “Now what?” because I watched a man throw his life away and I know deep within me nothing will really come of it. I cannot get into his head, either, and I can’t say for sure that he wanted to affect change. But I can’t read that manifesto and think that to some extent, he did. He empathised with Americans. He wanted better for them. I think he wanted change. But that change won’t really come. Actions that should be meaningful, actions that should change so much, end up changing nothing. Because our system — capitalism especially — is nihilistic.

A key aspect of capitalism in the way it has constructed itself is its capability to simply subsist. And it does so to frightening capacities. Capitalism has constructed itself in a way where it consistently fails but marches on. We live in an economic system that is capable of producing avatars who are willing to destroy the very planet they live on to keep said economic system alive, to keep it going, and to expand it. That should be very telling as to how deeply it can warp the psychologies of people. This is why I do not necessarily subscribe to the idea that we are approaching the death of capitalism. No, I think we just live on the death throes of capitalism, and that capitalism dies not before us, but after, when there are no more resources to exhaust. I titled this article “Living On a Corpse,” but that’s not entirely accurate, given that I think we are living in death throes that have yet to go quiet. A more accurate title, then, would be “Living On a Living Corpse” — a system of near-constant decay.

I call the system nihilistic because it feels like our actions within it seem to be constantly made meaningless. That’s not to say we can’t ever affect it, or undermine it, or reform it. But it feels like that is rarely ever the case, because its main method of offense (or defense) is to strip things of their meaning, such that it renders what we attempt to do within it null. The calculus of the death of capitalism itself is factored in — and is a product of — capitalism itself. This isn’t really my original thought here, but something that was set out by Jean Baudrillard in Simulacra and Simulation. Baudrillard argued that that capitalism leaves itself the “initiative of its own death,” which leaves it “the privileges of revolution,” and further argues that “the supreme ruse of the system, that of the simulacrum of its death, through which it maintains us in life by having liquidated through absorption all possible negativity” (Baudrillard, p.147). Revolutionary action is melted and swallowed into the system. Baudrillard continues, and you can see there what I echo here: “The system is itself also nihilistic, in the sense that it has the power to pour everything, including what denies it, into indifference” (Baudrillard, p.157).

I believe Baudrillard’s work in Simulacra and Simulation was incredibly prophetic and accurate to the state of things, and that was a book that was written and published decades ago, when things were not as bad as they are now. Things have gotten tangibly worse — and they only continue to do so.

I will end this section with the following excerpt:

“Death no longer has a stage, neither phantasmatic nor political, on which to represent itself, to play itself out, either a ceremonial or a violent one. And this is the victory of the other nihilism, of the other terrorism, that of the system.

There is no longer a stage, not even the minimal illusion that makes events capable of adopting the force of reality-no more stage either of mental or political solidarity: what do Chile, Biafra, the boat people, Bologna, or Poland matter? All of that comes to be annihilated on the television screen. We are in the era of events without consequences (and of theories without consequences)” (Baudrillard, p.157).

A Brutal Honesty

Part of me does have to think about what Luigi himself is thinking right now to the reception that he is getting from people specifically. When you look at his manifesto, it was pretty straightforward. There wasn’t necessarily a call to action. He simply ends it by saying: “Evidently I am the first to face it with such brutal honesty.” But I imagine that he probably wants some action, no? He at least does seem to believe that the public is somewhat culpable in their oppression, when he says “because the American public has allowed them to get away with it.” And I think, if I were in his shoes, I would be kind of pissed. Because what is going to come of it? He did face it with brutal honesty — one told through a bullet. And I don’t necessarily think he expected to change everything or to topple the system, nor do I necessarily think he expected to get people to the streets and for them to start killing other healthcare executives.

Martyrdom (when chosen) can be for many things. It can be to inspire awe, or fear, to express one’s grievances, it can be an act of defiance, it can be to capture minds through a spectacle. But I do think that while clearly Luigi has captured everyone’s minds, I don’t think we’re really going to do anything with it. A conversation was started, and I think it was very telling of the state of how people feel about things. But what comes next? I think as we watch the trial, we will probably be shifted in a certain way, but I don’t know how optimistic I am for that to manifest into anything meaningful (and meaningful here is incredibly vague). Just because Mangione didn’t singlehandedly topple the system doesn’t make his actions meaningless. But the level to which his actions will ultimately be nothing more than a conversation starter (one that is being wasted) truly does depress me. It did expose how a lot of people feel about the ruling class, which I think is important, but it also exposes how terribly we engage with things.

That feels like it adds to his martyrdom possibly being meaningless. It’s easy to blame billionaires or corporations or executives as those whose psychologies have been warped so deeply by said system and are damning us all. And it’s also easy to blame the system for being one that inherently strips things of meaning, which it absolutely does. And so you might say that what Luigi did was doomed from the start anyway, and you wouldn’t necessarily be wrong. But that’s not the full picture.

I titled a section earlier Three Miscalculations. However, I outlined only two miscalculations. The third miscalculation, I would argue, is that there really was no reason for the system to react in the way that it did. It only really gives the game away when the media all act in lockstep or when a mayor being investigated for corruption gets so intimately involved with the killing of a CEO. The third miscalculation was that not only would things be sorted out through neutralisation, it would be sorted out through us.

And so, that leads me to confront something myself with a brutal honesty: our psychologies have been very deeply warped, too. A staggering degree of apathy and meaninglessness permeates through — and wells within — all of us. Not that it would have necessarily been the right thing to do, but when we found out about the apocalyptic threat of climate change, and when we found out that our system had created and propped up units that were willing to doom us all to keep it alive, a rational response (but not necessarily a moral one) would have been to go to every single coal factory or oil refinery and tear it down and find every executive of Exxon or Shell or what have you and make them answer for sucking our planet dry. There is a level to which we have allowed ourselves to be victimised. Mangione himself somewhat faults the American people in his manifesto. The notion that they let the healthcare executives get away with things is one that ultimately points to a level of inaction that is inside people.

And the reason that we didn’t react as we did to climate change, and generally allow ourselves to be victimised to some extent, is because we do have bigger things to worry about than the end of the world (in the case of climate change). We have to worry about paying our rent and about affording our groceries and, ultimately, about our convenience, because without those things we spell the end of our worlds. I don’t say this facetiously, and I sympathise with that greatly. I do think that it is relatively fair to divest from the broader fate of the world because our day to day fates are so ethereal. Healthcare in America is a microcosm of this — while you might have a stable income and be relatively thriving, you also exist with the knowledge that if you get into a bad enough accident, you could end up watching all of that evaporate into mist. I can only fault people for their apathy so much, but I can also only excuse them (myself included) so much as well.

CONCLUSION

There are not many martyrs. I can’t ask nor expect that of people. I’m not one myself, nor do I aim to be, probably. The only thing I can ask of is that when we see one, we engage with them with the gravity they deserve. I firmly do not think that people engaged with Luigi Mangione with the gravity he deserved after doing what he did. He was at least engaged with, in ways that, say, Bushnell wasn’t, but it feels like an entirely wasted opportunity, for now. A serious conversation happened alongside a deeply unserious conversation, and quickly the latter became much louder. The other seemed to evaporate or be overshadowed. I hope that when the trial comes around, this won’t be the case. I’m not super optimistic, however. I also know that I personally don’t necessarily always engage with things with the gravity that they deserve. It’s often hard to. And I know how nihilistic this all reads. I don’t necessarily know the extent to which I fully believe how meaningless the world we live in is (in terms of how we can change it), but I am definitely more convinced in one direction than the other. It is at least the prevailing feeling I have right now. And that’s not to say that there’s nothing to be done. There absolutely is, even if in the grand scheme of things, I don’t know how much we can really change things. All I know is that we have to live to the best of our abilities right now, and that starts with trying to shed our apathy and combat the meaninglessness that dwells within us. Otherwise, we risk becoming reflections of the world we live in.

Living corpses, in constant decay.
