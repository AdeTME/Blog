The Mall

Introduction

In a country with an average year-round temperature of 28 degrees Celsius, social life in Qatar had nowhere to congregate but the mall. Both adolescent and familial social life congregated at the mall, across the economic classes that almost perfectly mapped onto ethnic lines. The following malls were the primary locales: Villaggio Mall and Hyatt Plaza, neighbours near Aspire Park. Landmark Mall, one of Qatar’s earliest modern malls. Centrepoint, the name of the main department store inside the mall, and the mall would be renamed to Al-Asmakh Mall, located in Al-Sadd, a neighbourhood in Qatar with a high concentration of cheaper apartment buildings, City Center, found in the West Bay, another one of its oldest and busiest malls. These were the malls that dominated the 2000s and early 2010s.

After finding parking in the lengthy parking lots necessitated by every mall and the availability of vast swathes of nothing, the most common gate of entry that a family uses to enter Villaggio is Gate Two, whose doors slide open and let you in to the one area in the mall that echoes, with a higher ceiling than the rest of the mall, painted the dark blue almost black of the night sky, with stars and clouds hardly ever seen in Doha. All the ceilings in Villaggio are painted like the sky, though it is daytime on those ceilings. Villaggio is one floor, but simulates the appearance of two floors, as above all the shop windows are constructed balconies that jut out ever so slightly, with windows that look into nothing, so that one cannot see the non-families that live above the Adidas or the Quiksilver, the American Eagle, Bershka, H&M, Mango, or Zara, across the mall floor from one of the largest grocery stores, Carrefour, next to a pharmacy, or above Paul, a French restaurant. The balconies also overlook the man-made blue river that runs through the mall, where one can ride a gondola for a few minutes to be taken from halfway across the mall to one of its ends near the food court, spitting you out at a GAP.

In Qatar’s Italian reconstruction there is a large food court that also houses a skating rink, and leads to an indoor theme park, Gondolania, which has a bowling alley on the first floor, a laser tag alley on the second floor, and a Go Kart track. There’s an arcade downstairs, and several rides. A one-and-a-half minute rollercoaster loops twice to make three minutes through the theme park. The food court is of two characters: the first is sit-down restaurants, TGI Friday’s, Outback Steakhouse, P.F. Chang’s, Nando’s, Shake-Shack, Applebee’s, the second is the main attraction, different fast food options and numerous tables, McDonald’s, KFC, Pizza Hut, Hardee’s, ice cream at a Baskin Robin’s, Krispy Kreme, or Coldstone. A family of five eats at the food court for roughly thirty Canadian Dollars, in the early 2010s. After, coffee but often tea and a pastry at Costa, Columbus, Starbucks, the coffee shops among others that were spaced throughout the mall, away from the food court.

This was the typical composition of the Qatari mall, all offering slightly different variations of the same experience. Hyatt Plaza, which housed Giant Stores that would be renamed Geant, was the quieter neighbour, less populated, not the location where young boys would go fight on Thursday after school (that was Villaggio). It housed another indoor themepark, the Jungle Zone. It was quieter, a Saturday (Middle Eastern weekends are Friday and Saturday) morning mall, for breakfast and groceries. Landmark was similar to it, though it had a cinema, an underground floor with a Kiddy Zone, a toystore, leading into the underground parking lot, and an arcade/theme park, Circus Land, though the actual circus happened a few times at the Hyatt Plaza, and often malls had shows happening in them, where people stood around a stage, and a young child watched a woman with a black bob in a sparkling silver blue bodysuit in complete enchantment, as she moved about stage, lithe with hula hoops, under Villaggio’s azure skies. Villaggio stood as king of the malls, and many mourned its four month closure when a nursery caught fire on the 28th of May, 2012, and a fire alarm that was too quiet meekly blared, killing thirteen children, four staff, and two firefighters, with the children being expatriates from Spain, New Zealand, France, Japan, and South Korea, and then Filipino immigrants, not expats. The nineteen deaths were the fault of a Nike nearby, and a lack of adherence to safety measures. There was a closed emergency door. It was a nursery that the Ministry of Social Affairs claimed had never been issued an operating license. The co-owners, Qatari husband from the Al-Thani family (the ruling tribe) and his Qatari wife (daughter of the Minister of Culture, Art, and Heritage at the time) were convicted alongside three others, one of them Villaggio’s chairman, whose attorney claimed had no shares in the mall. All were allowed to appeal and roam freely. It was also established that, since a civil employee licensed the nursery as an entertainment centre, its owners had less liabilities. All five convictions were overturned and dropped on the 26th of October in 2015. Abdulaziz bin Mohammad Al-Rabban, the Chairman, was fined five thousand five hundred dollars, and paid compensation to the families. The mall had long since reopened on September 20th, 2012.

Al-Asmakh Mall had a more distinctive ethnic or racial character to it — Egyptian and Indian, inverting the economic filter that Villaggio’s “luxury area” enacted. It was a more utilitarian mall, though it had an indoor amusement park. It was also in Al-Sadd, a cheaper district. Unlike Villaggio, Hyatt Plaza, and Landmark, it was a mall with multiple floors, as was the case with City Centre, one of Qatar’s busiest malls, with much to offer in terms of eating, entertainment, shopping, and another mall, the Royal Plaza, tall, recluse and unassuming, with a cinema of its own, though never with its fingers in Qatar’s social life. As the country industrialised further, more malls would materialise. Al-Mirqab Mall, in 2016, sprawling horizontally and vertically, Ezdan Mall, sometime before 2015, sprawling horizontally and vertically. These malls all somewhat break the naming conventions of Qatari malls, which all seemed to allude (in Villaggio’s case, declared) to the images they wished to construct and invoke, bustling hubs of wealth that imply activity or history, bustling hubs of wealth that imply wealth.

As a logical conclusion to the direction of the Qatari mall, The Mall of Qatar opened on the tenth of December, 2016. It was a bit farther out of the way from the rest of Doha, three floors, 464,515 square feet, the first floor housing a Cheesecake Factory that most of Qatar flocked to on opening day. A Cheesecake Factory opened in Villaggio soon after, following the success found at the Mall of Qatar. I don’t like massive crowds under certain conditions, and I didn’t like the direction that the mall had taken, but the day of its opening, my friends took me along. I followed my sense of curious perversion. The Mall was Mecca. Droves orbited the perimeter in cars, seeking a way in. Three floors necessitated multiple floors of parking, indoor and underground, and an above ground parking lot. Like most malls, Indian and sometimes African men wade between the parked Land Cruisers to retrieve the shopping carts left about in the lots with the express purpose of justifying the jobs of said men. Eventually, we found our way in with the crowds.

I don’t remember the gate we walked in through. There were simply too many. The ground floor was a collection of different sit-down restaurants, though I can only remember the Cheesecake Factory that had proudly planted its flag. They orbited a central area, where performances sometimes played. The former Crown Prince, Hamad bin Khalifa Al-Thani, was there for the mall’s baptism. Two-hundred and twenty stores attend, and so do thousands (a likely underestimation, or maybe just what felt like it) of people. There was a food court upstairs, a theatre, Adidas, Nike, downstairs, Tim Horton’s, Tommy Hilfiger, Lacoste, upstairs or downstairs, Armani Exchange, Bath and Body Works, Aldo, kiosks selling bukhoor or oud often worked by pushy North African salesmen with lined beards, Bershka, Boots, multiple grocery stores (Carrefour reappears), multiple jewelry stores, Geekay Games and Go Sport, Hollister, beauty lounges, toy stores, Pull & Bear, pharmacies, and a couple of modest bank offices. Restaurants, coffee shops, multiplied throughout, sometimes (as happened in some other malls, in all fairness) replicated at different locations in the same mall. It was one of the busier malls in Qatar moving forward. It also served to trim some of the fat of the economic classes. It was generally more expensive. And it was farther out, meaning the gas or taxis were more expensive, though slightly remedied by its eventual connection to Qatar’s metro that was built for the World Cup.

Following this logic was Doha Festival City, married to an IKEA that had opened years prioer, with the intention of being wed to Festival City. Festival City opened on the fifth of April, 2017. It was in Doha, though still somewhat farther out. It sprawled vertically and horizontally, multiple floors of parking lots, a massive parking lot shared between it and theIKEA, food court with all the typical franchises and more, hundreds of stores, fast-fashion and luxury brands all welcome under its glittering ceilings with no painted skies, a cinema, an Angry Birds-themed theme park, Angry Birds World, a Go Kart track (still Angry Birds themed) and Snow Dunes, an indoor snow park, grocery stores, tech stores, nothing necessarily new or unseen, but propagations, firm declarations that this tiny country in the Middle East with a native population of three hundred thousand or so (and two-and-a-half million immigrants) had made it.

The Mall of Qatar and Doha Festival City would be where, after being absent from Qatar for 565 days, on my return to Qatar I would see the newest addition to the Qatari mall: Indian and sometimes African men, in neon yellow vests, stationed about the mall. They are there to push the shopping carts of Qataris who no longer wished to do so. The grip that the mall had on Qatari social life has also been relatively loosened. While there were always outdoor areas to go, they are now bigger, larger, there are more parks, they have more amenities. Qatari social life was just as susceptible to cafe culture, as well. Standalone cafes, fancy, many locally-owned, now drew the floating men in white thobes and the flowing women in black abayas, and places like Katara, the Pearl, and most recently, the newly constructed Lusail City, or the pure luxury mall, Place Vendome, would more and more subsume Qatari social life. Food carts — entirely clean and sanitary, entirely contrary to the original intentions of the food cart — also became very popular, and many would drive by and order from their cars, never having to leave them for an Indian or Filipino man would come take their order and then hand it to them through the window of their cars. If they ever had to leave the cars, they were often left unlocked, with the keys still in the ignition. I left Qatar in 2019, as the mall was getting displaced. When I first moved to Montréal, I met some people, and they wanted to go out and do something. I recommended going to a mall. One of them asked me, “Why would we go to the mall?”

I “discovered” the Eaton Centre and the Place Montreal Trust alone. It’s mostly families, young people, or Arabs. Often two out of the three. I find myself in the dreariness of the Eaton Centre often. Once a month intentionally, more unintentionally. Complex Desjardins has yet to do much for me, though that might be for my lack of trying. Place Bonaventure and Place Ville Marie are entirely usurped by the finance men and women that work about, taking a break from selling lines of credit or credit card machines or some other ephemeral thing. The shops at Eaton, in the past six years that I’ve been here, have gotten brighter. The Timeout Market, the food court at Eaton, is dark, but not in any real way. The average meal there, though I have never had any, is probably twenty dollars.

I don’t know why I still go to the mall. I have not seen a woman with a black bob and a sparkling silver blue bodysuit with hula hoops at the mall in over a decade, now.
